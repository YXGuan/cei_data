***Q: Is there a centralized AI Governance Database that provides information on AI Governance from all religions?***  
---

**No, there is no single, centralized AI Governance Database that officially compiles AI governance frameworks from all world religions.**

Instead, the intersection of faith and artificial intelligence governance is tracked through a fractured network of independent multi-faith coalitions, academic projects, international bodies, and specific religious declarations.

If you are looking to track how different religions approach AI ethics and governance, you have to look at the primary databases, repositories, and frameworks maintained by these disparate organizations:

## 1\. The Rome Call for AI Ethics (The Vatican)

The closest initiative to a formalized, global, multi-faith framework is the **Rome Call for AI Ethics**. Originally issued by the Vatican’s Pontifical Academy for Life, this framework focuses on *"algorand-ethics"*—the ethical development of algorithms. \[1, 2, 3\]

* **Religion Coverage:** Originally Catholic, it has officially expanded via signing ceremonies to include major leadership from **Islam, Judaism, and Eastern religions (including Buddhism and Hinduism)**. \[4\]  
* **Where to find documentation:** You can read the core ethical requirements and list of global signatories directly on the official Rome Call for AI Ethics platform.

## 2\. Multi-Faith Think Tanks and Repositories

Because there is no centralized state-run repository, independent non-profits act as the primary database aggregators for multi-faith AI perspectives. \[5\]

* **AI and Faith:** This is an organization that builds a community of cross-disciplinary experts to integrate faith-based perspectives into AI governance. They frequently track and contribute to global policy structures, including the [United Nations AI Advisory Body](https://aiandfaith.org/news/ai-and-faith-role-in-uns-new-landmark-ai-governance-report/) reports. Their site functions as a hub for articles, white papers, and cross-religious AI viewpoints. \[6, 7, 8, 9, 10\]  
* **The Boston Global Forum:** This think tank tracks spiritual values for "The Knowledge Platform for AI," mapping how specific concepts—like Catholic teachings on [Human Dignity (*Dignitatis Humanae*)](https://bostonglobalforum.org/news/spiritual-values-of-religions-for-the-knowledge-platform-for-ai/) or Buddhist concepts of mindfulness—can be integrated into technical AI decision-making.

## 3\. Individual Religious Declarations

If you are looking for specific doctrine-based datasets, major religious bodies have published individual frameworks:

* **Evangelical Christianity:** The Ethics & Religious Liberty Commission published the comprehensive [Artificial Intelligence: An Evangelical Statement of Principles](https://erlc.com/policy-content/artificial-intelligence-an-evangelical-statement-of-principles/), which outlines boundaries on human dignity, work, and data privacy from a conservative Protestant perspective. \[11, 12\]  
* **The Lausanne Movement:** Offers technical standards and ethical frameworks for [Governing AI in God's House](https://lausanne.org/global-analysis/governing-ai-in-gods-house), blending Christian stewardship with modern data protection. \[13\]

## Where to Look for Standardized Databases

If you require strict, structured data tracking for AI policy, you will have to use standard secular policy databases and manually filter for religious or ethical criteria. The most reliable tools for this include:

* The [OECD AI Policy Observatory](https://oecd.ai/en/), which tracks national AI strategies and ethical frameworks globally.  
* The [NIST AI Risk Management Framework (AI RMF)](https://www.bradley.com/insights/publications/2025/08/global-ai-governance-five-key-frameworks-explained), which provides structural guidelines often used by faith-based organizations to map out bias and fairness tracking. \[14, 15, 16\]
